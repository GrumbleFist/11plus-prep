# 11+ Preparation: Evidence-Based Best Practices

## Learning Science Foundations

### Spaced Repetition
One of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology. Spacing practice over time produces significantly better retention than massed practice. Questions should recur at increasing intervals (Leitner/SM-2 algorithm).

### Retrieval Practice (Testing Effect)
Actively recalling from memory strengthens memory far more than re-reading. Tool should be question-first, not lesson-first. Even incorrect attempts prime the brain for learning from feedback.

### Interleaving
Mixing different problem types within a session (e.g., fractions → VR → percentages) forces deeper processing. Research: ~63% accuracy on interleaved vs lower on blocked content.

### Worked Examples (Cognitive Load Theory)
Novice learners benefit enormously from studying step-by-step worked examples. Reduces extraneous cognitive load.
**Expertise Reversal Effect**: Worked examples become counterproductive as proficiency grows. System should fade scaffolding as competence increases.

### Desirable Difficulties (Bjork, 1994)
Making learning somewhat harder short-term leads to stronger long-term retention. Spacing, interleaving, and retrieval practice are all desirable difficulties.

### Rosenshine's Principles
- Present new material in small steps
- Provide many worked examples
- Ask many questions
- Provide scaffolds for difficult tasks
- Require and monitor independent practice
- Conduct weekly/monthly review
- Effect sizes: scaffolding d=0.82, feedback d=0.70, worked examples d=0.57

### Piaget: Concrete Operational Stage (age 7-11)
10-year-olds think logically about concrete situations but struggle with purely abstract thinking. Explanations should use concrete, tangible examples — not abstract definitions.

## Explanation Design for 10-Year-Olds

### Language
- Simple, direct sentences
- Define technical terms using words child already knows
- Year 5 reading level — multi-syllable OK but plain language preferred

### Analogies
- Connect to real experience: shopping (percentages), cooking (fractions/ratios), sports (statistics)
- Concrete before abstract: "A fraction is like cutting a pizza"

### Structure
1. State what the question asks in plain language
2. Show first step + explain why
3. Each subsequent step with reasoning
4. Highlight the answer
5. State the general rule/pattern
6. Offer a follow-up "try this" question

### Visual Aids
- Diagrams, number lines, bar models reduce cognitive load
- Colour coding for multi-step solutions
- For NVR: highlight the changing element

## Gamification (What Works)

### Evidence
Meta-analysis (41 studies, 5,071+ participants): large overall effect (g=0.822) for gamification in education. But: higher engagement ≠ higher achievement automatically. Effects can decline over time.

### Effective Elements
- **Streaks**: daily practice streaks (keep requirements achievable)
- **Points/XP**: for completing questions, bonuses for accuracy
- **Levels**: clear progression ("Level 34 in Maths")
- **Badges**: for genuine milestones, not just participation
- **Progress bars**: visual fill toward topic mastery

### Avoid
- Punitive mechanics (losing points for wrong answers)
- Overly complex reward systems
- Social comparison that shames weaker performers
- Over-praising — "Correct" is sufficient for routine questions

## Difficulty Calibration

### The 85% Rule (Wilson et al., Nature Communications 2019)
Learning optimised when learner succeeds ~85% of the time. Target this accuracy rate; if >90% increase difficulty, if <75% ease back.

### Zone of Proximal Development (Vygotsky)
Effective learning happens between what child can do independently and what they can do with guidance. Too easy = no growth; too hard = frustration.

### 100 Levels Structure
- **Levels 1-20**: Year 4 content, high scaffolding, many worked examples
- **Levels 21-50**: Year 5 content, increasing complexity, reduced scaffolding
- **Levels 51-80**: Year 5/6 + 11+ specific question types, mixed/interleaved
- **Levels 81-100**: Exam-level difficulty, timed conditions, challenging edge cases

### Difficulty Variables
- Number of steps required
- Vocabulary complexity
- Time pressure (introduce gradually)
- Distractor quality (more plausible at higher levels)
- Scaffolding amount (more at lower, fading at higher)
- Problem novelty (familiar → unfamiliar applications)

## Feedback Design

### After Wrong Answer
1. State clearly incorrect — neutral tone ("Not quite" not "Wrong!")
2. Show correct answer immediately
3. Step-by-step worked example
4. Explain likely misconception
5. Offer follow-up question of same type
6. More scaffolding for lower-knowledge children

### After Right Answer
1. Brief confirmation ("Correct!" or checkmark)
2. Easy questions: no additional explanation, move on
3. Hard questions: optionally show solution method
4. Award points/XP
5. Don't over-praise — reserve enthusiasm for genuine achievements

### Research: Immediate feedback (after each question) most effective for children. 44% more correct answers vs delayed.

## Session Design

### Duration
- Target: 20-30 minutes focused practice
- Structure: 2 blocks of 10-15 min with 2-3 min break
- Maximum productive: 45-50 min with breaks
- Minimum useful: 10 minutes

### Frequency
- Optimal: daily short sessions (15-20 min), 5-6 days/week
- At least one full rest day
- Final 4-6 weeks: can increase to 30-40 min daily

### Burnout Prevention
- Set daily time limits (hard stop at 40 min, warning at 30)
- Track consecutive days, prompt rest after 10-12 days
- Vary question types within sessions
- Allow child some autonomy in choosing what to practice
- Celebrate effort and improvement, not just scores

## Progress Tracking

### For Parents
1. Overall readiness score vs target school's pass mark
2. Topic-level heatmap (Strong/Needs Work/Improving)
3. Trend over time (weekly average line chart)
4. Practice consistency (days/week, streaks)
5. Time spent
6. Comparison benchmarks (vs others targeting same school)
7. Top 3-5 weakest areas with links to practice

### For Child
1. Level/XP with clear progression
2. Streak counter with visual calendar
3. Stars/mastery per topic (1-5)
4. Recent badges/achievements
5. Progress bar to next milestone
6. "You've improved!" indicators

### Visualisation
- Translate to plain language ("on track" not "SAS: 112.4")
- Traffic light colour coding (green/amber/red)
- Max 5-7 data points visible, drill-down available
- Show both absolute performance AND rate of improvement
